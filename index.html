How much the S&P 500 makes — and how much reaches its workers

Fair500 maps the profitability and pay fairness of every company in the S&P 500 — from Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet (Google), Nvidia and Meta to Walmart, JPMorgan Chase, ExxonMobil, Coca-Cola, McDonald's and Disney. For each of 462 companies it plots how much profit the business earns against how much of that money actually reaches the people who do the work.

The picture is built from two figures every large company must disclose: the CEO-to-worker pay ratio — how many times the median employee's pay the chief executive takes home — and median worker pay measured against the profit each employee generates. Together they turn the abstract debate over executive compensation and income inequality into a concrete, company-by-company comparison, sourced entirely from SEC filings.

Companies with large hourly and part-time workforces — retailers and restaurant chains such as Starbucks, Target, Costco and McDonald's — often show the widest pay ratios, while capital-intensive and high-margin businesses concentrate profit among far fewer employees. Frequently asked questions

What is the CEO-to-worker pay ratio?

Since 2018, every U.S. public company has had to disclose the ratio of its CEO's total annual compensation to that of its median employee, under Section 953(b) of the Dodd-Frank Act. A ratio of 300-to-1 means the chief executive is paid about 300 times what a typical worker at that company earns. Fair500 reads this figure straight from each company's annual proxy statement (SEC form DEF 14A).

How does Fair500 measure pay fairness?

Each company receives a 0–100 fairness score built from two equally weighted lenses: the CEO-to-worker pay gap, and median worker pay relative to the profit each employee helps generate. You can view either lens on its own or combined. A higher score means more of the company's financial success reaches the people who work there.

Which S&P 500 companies have the widest CEO-to-worker pay gaps?

The widest ratios tend to belong to companies that employ large numbers of part-time and hourly staff — big retailers, restaurant and hospitality chains such as Starbucks, Coca-Cola, TJX and McDonald's — where a modest median wage sits beneath a large executive pay package. Switch to the 'CEO Pay Gap' view and sort the table to see the current ranking.

Where does Fair500's data come from?

Every figure is pulled from primary SEC filings. Profit and revenue come from EDGAR XBRL financial data for the last three fiscal years; the pay ratio, median worker pay and employee headcount come from each company's DEF 14A proxy statement and 10-K annual report. Nothing is estimated where a company discloses the real number.

How often is the data updated?

The dataset is rebuilt as companies file their annual proxy statements, which cluster in the spring each year. Each company's numbers reflect its three most recent fiscal years. Last updated July 2026.

Is Fair500 free to use?

Yes. Fair500 is free and supported by advertising. It draws on public government data and is not affiliated with the SEC or with any company listed.
